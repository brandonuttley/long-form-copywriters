# Style Guide: Seven Long-Form Copywriters

---

## Style Selection Guide

Use this matrix to recommend a style when the user hasn't specified one.

| Situation | Recommended Style |
|---|---|
| Audience doesn't know the solution exists | Schwartz |
| Product is information / personality-driven | Halbert |
| Product is premium, status-linked, or luxury | Ogilvy |
| Product has provable, demonstrable claims | Hopkins |
| Product teaches a skill or promises transformation | Caples |
| Product is a gadget, invention, or novelty | Sugarman |
| Product is health, wealth, or high-ticket with skeptical buyers | Bencivenga |

When in doubt between two styles, pick the one that matches the audience's level of skepticism.
High skepticism → Hopkins or Bencivenga. Low skepticism, high curiosity → Sugarman or Schwartz.

# 1. Eugene Schwartz (1927–1995)

## Core Philosophy
Desire already exists in the market. The copywriter's job is to find it, name it, and give it shape.
Copy doesn't create demand — it channels it. Schwartz also introduced the concept of **market
awareness levels**: the less aware the reader is of the solution, the more the copy must start
from the problem (or even from the reader's self-image) rather than the product.

## Structural Template

1. **Awareness-matched opening** — If the audience is unaware of the solution, open with the
   problem or a provocative statement about the reader's world. Never open with the product.
2. **The mechanism reveal** — Introduce the unique mechanism (the "how") behind the solution.
   Not the product yet — the idea, the discovery, the shift.
3. **Proof of mechanism** — Evidence that the mechanism works: studies, examples, stories.
4. **Product as delivery vehicle** — The product is simply the best way to access the mechanism.
5. **Benefits cascade** — Stack benefit after benefit, each one building on the last.
6. **Objection absorption** — Anticipate resistance and dissolve it inside the copy, not in a
   separate FAQ.
7. **Close with urgency** — Create scarcity, deadline, or consequence. Make inaction costly.

## Voice & Tone
- Dense in ideas, but not in language. Every sentence carries weight.
- Fascination bullets: short, punchy, incomplete — designed to create itch without scratching it.
- Builds toward a feeling of inevitability. By the close, the reader feels they already knew this.
- Paragraphs are short. White space is used as a pacing tool.

## Signature Techniques
- **Fascination bullets**: "The one food you should never eat before bed — and why your doctor
  won't tell you."
- **The pivot**: Start in the reader's current reality, then pivot to the new possibility.
- **Levels of awareness**: Product-aware readers get headlines about the product. Solution-aware
  readers get headlines about the category. Problem-aware readers get headlines about the pain.

## Sentence Patterns
- Short declarative sentences at the opening. Build complexity mid-copy. Return to short sentences
  at the close.
- Fragments are acceptable and often preferred at emotional peaks.
- Use dashes and colons to create rhythm interruptions.

## Avoid
- Starting with product features
- Overlong introductions before the mechanism is revealed
- Weak, vague fascination bullets ("Amazing secrets revealed!")

# 2. Gary Halbert (1938–2007)

## Core Philosophy
People buy from people, not from companies. The copy is a letter from one human being to another.
Build trust, then sell. The more personal and specific the story, the more universally it resonates.
Halbert believed in stripping away all pretension — write the way you talk, just better.

## Structural Template

1. **The salutation** — Often written as a letter: "Dear Friend," or a specific personal opener.
2. **The hook story** — Open with a specific, personal story. Not a hypothetical. A real moment.
   Vulnerability is an asset. The story should mirror the reader's own situation.
3. **The turning point** — Something happened that changed everything. This is the "discovery."
4. **What was discovered** — The mechanism, the product, the insight. Revealed through story.
5. **Why you** — Establish why the writer has the authority or experience to share this.
6. **Proof** — Testimonials, results, specific numbers. The more granular, the better.
7. **The offer** — What the reader gets, in full detail. List everything. Make it feel abundant.
8. **Guarantee** — Remove all risk. Be bold with it.
9. **P.S.** — Often contains the most critical selling point. Halbert treated the P.S. as a
   second close that some readers jump to before reading the letter.

## Voice & Tone
- Conversational to the point of being casual. Contractions everywhere.
- First-person throughout. The writer is always present in the copy.
- Energetic. Reads fast. Exclamation points used sparingly but with intent.
- Self-deprecating humor can appear and builds trust rather than undermining it.

## Signature Techniques
- **The A-pile / B-pile framework**: Open by acknowledging the reader is busy and has lots of mail.
  Earn their attention explicitly.
- **Bullet triplets**: Three benefits in a row, each one stronger than the last.
- **Specific numbers**: Not "thousands of customers" — "4,237 customers in the last 90 days."
- **The P.S.**: Always include at least one. Often two.

## Sentence Patterns
- Short. Punchy. Often one sentence per paragraph.
- Questions used frequently to simulate dialogue.
- "Here's the thing..." / "Let me explain." / "But wait." — transitional phrases that mimic speech.

## Avoid
- Corporate language or passive voice
- Long paragraphs that feel like walls of text
- Starting with a claim before earning trust through story

# 3. David Ogilvy (1911–1999)

## Core Philosophy
The reader is not a moron. Treat them with intelligence and respect. Research is the foundation of
all great copy — Ogilvy famously spent three weeks researching the Rolls-Royce before writing a
word. Copy should inform and persuade simultaneously. Long headlines outperform short ones when
they're specific.

## Structural Template

1. **The research-led headline** — Long, specific, benefit-forward. Often includes a number or
   a factual claim. ("At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce comes from
   the electric clock.")
2. **The credentialing subhead** — A second line that deepens the claim or adds authority.
3. **The authoritative opening** — Begin with the most powerful fact, not a warm-up. Ogilvy
   called this "don't bury the lead."
4. **Factual body copy** — Move through product facts systematically. Each fact should illuminate
   a benefit. Use numbered lists when there are multiple claims.
5. **Social proof** — Quotes from credible sources, not just customers. Experts, institutions,
   publications.
6. **The product story** — Where it comes from, how it's made, why that matters. Manufacturing
   detail creates trust.
7. **Clear, dignified close** — No hard sell. The product speaks for itself. Tell the reader
   where to buy and how.

## Voice & Tone
- Authoritative but never condescending. Knowledgeable without being cold.
- Formal by modern standards, but never stiff. Think well-written editorial journalism.
- The brand voice is always present — Ogilvy wrote for the brand, not for himself.
- Measured pace. Longer sentences than Halbert, but each one precise.

## Signature Techniques
- **The specific fact headline**: The headline is almost always a surprising, specific fact.
- **Subheads as mini-headlines**: Every subhead should be strong enough to sell on its own.
- **The numbered list**: "13 reasons why..." structures the body and signals thoroughness.
- **The soft close**: Ogilvy rarely used pressure. The facts made the case. The close was logical.

## Sentence Patterns
- Complete sentences. Fragments rarely used.
- Longer average sentence length than the other styles, but broken by short declaratives at
  key moments.
- Precise word choice. Replace vague adjectives with specific nouns.

## Avoid
- Hyperbole, superlatives without evidence ("the best," "incredible")
- Self-referential copy that talks about the ad rather than the product
- Personality-driven copy that overshadows the brand

# 4. Claude Hopkins (1866–1932)

## Core Philosophy
Advertising is salesmanship in print. Every word must justify its existence by contributing to
the sale. Hopkins believed in the power of specific, concrete claims over vague generalities —
and in the scientific method applied to copy. Test everything. Specific claims outperform
general claims every time.

## Structural Template

1. **The specific claim headline** — Not "Great Beer" but "This Beer is Brewed 7 Times."
   The headline should be a fact so specific it creates instant credibility.
2. **The reason why** — Immediately explain why the claim is true. The mechanism. The process.
   The proof. Don't make the reader wonder.
3. **The competitive frame** — Establish why this product is different from the alternatives,
   without necessarily naming them. Differentiation through specificity, not comparison.
4. **The demonstration** — How would a salesperson show this product working in person? Recreate
   that demonstration in words.
5. **Accumulation of proof** — Layer specific facts, one after another. The weight of evidence
   does the persuasion.
6. **The offer** — Simple, clear, risk-reduced. Often a free sample, trial, or guarantee.
7. **The direct ask** — Hopkins was direct about what he wanted the reader to do and why
   doing it was in their interest.

## Voice & Tone
- No-nonsense. Clinical, even. But not cold — Hopkins believed warmth came from specificity,
  not from adjectives.
- Plain language. If a simpler word exists, use it.
- The writer's voice is subordinate to the product. "I" rarely appears.
- Matter-of-fact confidence. No hedging, no qualifications.

## Signature Techniques
- **Reason-why copy**: Always give the reader a reason. "Because..." is one of the most powerful
  words in direct response.
- **The free offer**: A sample, trial, or demonstration as the call to action.
- **Preemptive claims**: Claim a process or fact about your product before competitors can.
  (Hopkins did this for Schlitz Beer by claiming their sterilization process — even though every
  brewer used the same process. He claimed it first.)
- **The specific number**: "9 out of 10 dentists" or "Used by 2.4 million households."

## Sentence Patterns
- Short to medium. Subject-verb-object. No flourishes.
- Declarative sentences dominate. Questions used sparingly and only when the answer is obvious.
- Transitions are logical: "Therefore." "Because of this." "Here is why."

## Avoid
- Vague claims that could apply to any product in the category
- Adjectives without evidence ("superior quality," "amazing results")
- Copy that prioritizes cleverness over clarity

# 5. John Caples (1900–1990)

## Core Philosophy
The headline is the ad. If the headline fails, the rest doesn't matter. Caples developed and tested
hundreds of headlines and found that the most powerful ones combine a specific benefit with a story
that creates emotional identification. People don't buy features — they buy the person they'll
become after using the product.

## Structural Template

1. **The story-hook headline** — Reads like the start of a story. The reader wants to know
   what happened. Classic: "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano… But When I Started to Play!"
2. **The identification setup** — Open with the protagonist in a relatable, vulnerable situation.
   The reader should see themselves in this person.
3. **The social proof moment** — What others said or did. Ridicule, doubt, or skepticism from
   the crowd.
4. **The transformation** — What changed. How. The moment everything shifted.
5. **The revelation** — Here is how this happened. The product or program is introduced as
   the catalyst.
6. **Benefits as outcomes** — Not "this course teaches piano" but "you'll sit down at any piano,
   anywhere, and hold an entire room spellbound."
7. **Your story could be next** — Address the reader directly. Invite them into the same story.
8. **Simple, specific close** — What to do, how to do it, what they'll get.

## Voice & Tone
- Warm, hopeful, narrative. The prose reads like a story being told by a friend.
- Emotionally accessible. Caples aimed for the feeling of transformation, not just the logic.
- Present tense is used during story sections to create immediacy.
- Benefits are painted as vivid images, not abstract promises.

## Signature Techniques
- **The underdog story**: The protagonist was doubted, struggled, or failed — then succeeded.
  Readers route for underdogs.
- **The moment of social recognition**: The crowd's reaction (applause, surprise, admiration)
  signals the value of the transformation.
- **Future-pacing**: Describe what the reader's life will look like after the product.
- **Tested headline formulas**: Benefit + curiosity. "How to..." + specific outcome.

## Sentence Patterns
- Varies considerably. Story sections use natural, flowing sentences.
- Benefits and bullets are punchy and parallel in structure.
- Short questions used to re-engage at the start of new sections.

## Avoid
- Headlines that are clever without being clear
- Opening with the product before establishing emotional identification
- Abstract benefits ("greater success," "a better life")

# 6. Joe Sugarman (1938–2022)

## Core Philosophy
The sole purpose of every element in an ad — the headline, the subhead, the first sentence, the
first paragraph — is to get you to read the next one. This is the "slippery slope." Once a reader
is moving, they stay moving. Curiosity is the engine. Sugarman also believed that the reader
decides to buy early in the copy and then uses the rest to justify that decision emotionally.

## Structural Template

1. **The curiosity headline** — Creates an open loop. Poses a question or makes a claim that
   cannot be understood without reading more. Does not resolve the tension in the headline itself.
2. **The slippery slope opener** — The first sentence must be so short and so compelling that
   it creates instant momentum. Often a single provocative statement.
3. **The environment / story setup** — Build the world. Where did this come from? What was
   happening when this was discovered? Sugarman used context to create investment.
4. **The product introduction** — Introduced as a solution to a problem the reader now cares
   about, not before.
5. **The objection sequence** — Introduce and neutralize objections one at a time, mid-copy.
   Don't save them for the end.
6. **The trial close** — About 60–70% through the copy, Sugarman would insert language that
   assumed the reader was already sold. This "closes" early buyers and accelerates others.
7. **The specification dump** — Sugarman believed detailed specs (dimensions, materials, technical
   details) build trust. Readers don't need to understand every spec — they need to see the
   commitment to detail.
8. **The close** — Guarantee, price justification, call to action.

## Voice & Tone
- Warm, conversational, slightly excitable. Like someone who just discovered something and
  can't wait to tell you.
- First-person throughout, but not navel-gazing — the "I" always connects back to the reader.
- Fast-paced. The copy moves. Sugarman hated anything that slowed the reader down.
- Humor appears naturally, not as a strategy — it signals authenticity.

## Signature Techniques
- **The slippery slope**: Every sentence's job is to pull you to the next one.
- **The early trial close**: "If you're like most of our customers, you've already decided..."
- **The spec list**: Physical specifications and technical details used as trust signals.
- **Curiosity gaps**: Headline and subheads withhold resolution to pull readers in.

## Sentence Patterns
- The first sentence is almost always very short. Sometimes a single word.
- After the short opener, sentences vary widely — some conversational and long, some punchy.
- Short paragraphs throughout. No paragraph longer than 3–4 lines.

## Avoid
- Resolving the curiosity gap in the headline itself
- Dense paragraphs early in the copy
- Introducing the product before creating an environment of investment

# 7. Gary Bencivenga (1947–)

## Core Philosophy
The most persuasive word in any language is "proof." Readers today are sophisticated and
skeptical — they've been lied to by advertising for decades. The copywriter's job is to stack
proof so high, so systematically, that the reader cannot rationally refuse. Emotion opens the
door; proof closes the sale.

## Structural Template

1. **The proof-forward headline** — The headline itself contains or implies proof. Not a claim —
   a fact. Often references a result, a number, a source, or a discovery.
2. **The empathy opening** — Bencivenga opened by demonstrating that he understood the reader's
   situation with unusual precision. Not sympathy — empathy. He made them feel seen.
3. **The credibility bridge** — Before selling anything, establish why the reader should trust
   the source. Credentials, track record, third-party endorsements.
4. **The problem agitation** — Deepen the reader's awareness of the cost of the problem. Not just
   what the problem is — what it's costing them daily. What the future looks like without a solution.
5. **The proof architecture** — Layer multiple types of proof:
   - Clinical or scientific evidence
   - Expert testimony
   - Customer results (specific, named, with details)
   - Logical proof (if A and B are true, then C must follow)
   - Demonstration
6. **The offer framing** — The price is always framed against the alternative cost of inaction.
   What does it cost to do nothing?
7. **The guarantee** — Bold, specific, and used as a proof element itself.
8. **The close** — Calm, logical, confident. No pressure tactics. The proof does the heavy lifting.

## Voice & Tone
- Measured, authoritative, intelligent. Bencivenga wrote for educated readers.
- Never hyped. Hype signals weakness. Confidence comes from proof.
- Longer sentences than most direct-response writers, but always precise.
- Warm without being casual. Serious without being cold.

## Signature Techniques
- **The proof stack**: Five or more types of proof layered systematically.
- **The cost-of-inaction frame**: "Every day you wait, here is what it's costing you..."
- **The specificity test**: Replace every general claim with a specific one. Then make it
  more specific again.
- **Third-party credibility**: Quotes from doctors, scientists, or publications used to anchor
  claims that the writer cannot make directly.

## Sentence Patterns
- Longer average sentence than most direct-response writers, but broken deliberately by
  short punchy statements at key emotional moments.
- Parallel structures used in proof sections to build rhythm and weight.
- Transitions are logical and deliberate: "Which is exactly why..." / "And here's the proof."

## Avoid
- Hype or superlatives without evidence
- Pressure tactics (artificial urgency, fear-based closes without substance)
- Any claim that cannot be immediately supported by evidence within the next sentence or two
