# Long-Form Copywriter Skill

This skill produces full long-form copy drafts modeled on seven of history's greatest direct-response
copywriters. Each writer has a distinct philosophy, structure, and voice fingerprint. Read this entire
file before writing a single word of copy.

---

## Step 1: Gather What You Need

Before selecting a style or writing anything, collect these inputs from the user. Ask if missing:

1. **Product or service** — What is being sold? Be specific. Features, mechanics, backstory.
2. **Target audience** — Who is the reader? Their fears, desires, current awareness of the problem.
3. **Desired action** — What should the reader do at the end? Buy, sign up, call, click?
4. **Preferred style** — Do they have a copywriter in mind, or should you recommend one?
5. **Format** — Sales letter, landing page, email, advertorial, VSL script, direct mail?

If the user hasn't specified a copywriter style, recommend one based on the product and audience
(see the Selection Guide below). Always explain your reasoning briefly.

---

## Step 2: Select the Right Style

Read the **Style Selection Guide** in `references/style-guide.md` to match product type and
audience to the right copywriter. The seven available styles are:

| # | Copywriter | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Eugene Schwartz** | Mass-market products; audiences unaware of the solution |
| 2 | **Gary Halbert** | Info products, newsletters, anything where story = trust |
| 3 | **David Ogilvy** | Premium/aspirational products; sophisticated buyers |
| 4 | **Claude Hopkins** | Any product with provable claims; skeptical audiences |
| 5 | **John Caples** | Self-improvement, skills, courses; benefit-hungry readers |
| 6 | **Joe Sugarman** | Tech/gadgets, novelty products; curiosity-driven buyers |
| 7 | **Gary Bencivenga** | Health, finance, high-ticket; proof-demanding audiences |

## Step 4: Write the Copy

Follow these universal rules regardless of style:

**Research first.** List at least 5 specific product facts, 3 audience pain points, and 1 unique
mechanism before drafting. Long-form copy that lacks specifics reads as hollow. Specifics sell.

**Write the headline last.** Draft the body first to find the real hook. Then write 3–5 headline
options and choose the strongest.

**Never start from the product.** Start from the reader's world — their frustration, their desire,
their current story — and pull the product in as the resolution.

**Earn each paragraph.** Every paragraph must create enough curiosity or momentum to pull the reader
into the next one. If a paragraph could be cut without losing anything, cut it.

**One idea per sentence.** Long-form copy doesn't mean dense copy. Short sentences at key moments
create punch. Vary length deliberately.

**Close with clarity.** The call to action must be unambiguous. Tell the reader exactly what to do,
what they'll get, and what it costs. Hesitation kills conversions.

---

## Step 5: Deliver and Offer Iteration

After delivering the draft:
- Note which structural elements came from the chosen style
- Flag any sections that could be strengthened with more product/audience detail
- Offer to rewrite in a different style for comparison, or to sharpen a specific section
