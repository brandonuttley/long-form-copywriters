# Style Evaluation Rubric

Use this rubric to assess whether a draft genuinely matches the target style — or whether it's
drifted into generic AI copy wearing a label.

For each style, check the structural requirements, voice characteristics, and sentence patterns.
Then run the failure mode check. If more than two failure modes are present, the draft needs revision.

A passing draft should hit all structural requirements and at least 4 of 5 voice characteristics.

---

## Universal Checks (Apply to Every Style)

Before checking style-specific criteria, verify these apply to any draft:

- [ ] The copy does NOT open with the product name or a product feature
- [ ] The headline was written last and chosen from at least 3 options
- [ ] Every paragraph earns the next — no paragraph exists merely to fill space
- [ ] At least 3 specific facts, numbers, or details appear (not vague claims)
- [ ] The call to action names the exact action, the exact outcome, and the exact price
- [ ] Sentence length varies — there is no uniform rhythm throughout

If any universal check fails, revise before checking style-specific criteria.

## Style 1: Eugene Schwartz

### Structural Requirements
- [ ] Opening matches the audience's awareness level (problem-first for unaware audiences;
  solution-first only if audience is already solution-aware)
- [ ] The "mechanism" — the how behind the result — is named and explained before the product
- [ ] Fascination bullets appear (at least 5, each creating curiosity without resolving it)
- [ ] Objections are addressed inside the copy, not in a separate FAQ section
- [ ] The close creates a cost to inaction, not just a benefit to acting

### Voice Characteristics
- [ ] Short paragraphs (3 lines or fewer in most cases)
- [ ] Fragments appear at emotional peaks
- [ ] Bullets are incomplete — they create an itch the reader can only scratch by buying
- [ ] The reader's desire is named and channeled, not manufactured
- [ ] By the close, the outcome feels inevitable rather than sold

### Failure Modes to Check
- [ ] FAIL if the product is introduced before the mechanism
- [ ] FAIL if bullets resolve their own curiosity ("The secret to X — and it's Y")
- [ ] FAIL if the opening addresses the product rather than the reader's world
- [ ] FAIL if any paragraph exceeds 5 lines without a break
- [ ] FAIL if the urgency in the close is artificial (countdown timer language with no real reason)

## Style 2: Gary Halbert

### Structural Requirements
- [ ] Opens with a salutation or direct personal address ("Dear Friend," or equivalent)
- [ ] A specific, personal story appears in the first 20% of the copy
- [ ] The story contains a moment of vulnerability or failure before the turning point
- [ ] The product/solution is introduced through the story, not before it
- [ ] At least one P.S. is present, and it contains a meaningful selling point (not just a
  restatement of the offer)

### Voice Characteristics
- [ ] Conversational — contractions throughout, no formal constructions
- [ ] First-person throughout — the writer is always present
- [ ] Specific numbers replace general claims ("4,237 customers" not "thousands")
- [ ] Questions appear regularly to simulate dialogue
- [ ] Short paragraphs — most 1–3 sentences

### Failure Modes to Check
- [ ] FAIL if the copy opens with a claim rather than a story
- [ ] FAIL if no P.S. is present
- [ ] FAIL if numbers are rounded or vague ("about 4,000" vs "4,237")
- [ ] FAIL if the writer's voice disappears in the middle sections
- [ ] FAIL if the story's protagonist is not relatable to the target reader

## Style 3: David Ogilvy

### Structural Requirements
- [ ] Headline is specific, factual, and contains either a number or a surprising claim
- [ ] A credentialing subhead follows the headline
- [ ] The opening paragraph contains the most powerful fact — not a warm-up
- [ ] Body copy moves through product facts systematically with each fact linked to a benefit
- [ ] Social proof comes from credible sources (experts, publications, institutions) not just
  customers
- [ ] The close is dignified — no pressure tactics, no artificial urgency

### Voice Characteristics
- [ ] Complete sentences throughout — fragments are rare
- [ ] Authoritative but not condescending
- [ ] The brand voice is present — copy serves the product, not the writer's personality
- [ ] Vague adjectives ("superior," "amazing") are replaced by specific nouns and facts
- [ ] Longer average sentence length than Halbert or Sugarman, broken by short declaratives
  at key moments

### Failure Modes to Check
- [ ] FAIL if the headline is clever without being informative
- [ ] FAIL if superlatives appear without evidence ("the finest," "unparalleled")
- [ ] FAIL if the copy refers to itself ("this ad," "what you're reading")
- [ ] FAIL if the close uses pressure ("Act now before it's too late")
- [ ] FAIL if the opening warm-up delays the strongest fact past the second paragraph

## Style 4: Claude Hopkins

### Structural Requirements
- [ ] Headline contains a specific, verifiable claim (not a vague promise)
- [ ] The claim in the headline is immediately explained with a "reason why" in the first
  paragraph
- [ ] A competitive frame appears — why this product differs, without naming competitors
- [ ] Proof accumulates throughout — each paragraph adds to the weight of evidence
- [ ] The offer includes a risk-reduction element (free trial, guarantee, or sample)

### Voice Characteristics
- [ ] Plain language throughout — no word used when a simpler word exists
- [ ] Matter-of-fact confidence — no hedging, no qualifications
- [ ] The writer's "I" rarely appears — the product is the subject
- [ ] Transitions are logical: "Therefore," "Because of this," "Here is why"
- [ ] Short to medium sentences — subject-verb-object, no flourishes

### Failure Modes to Check
- [ ] FAIL if any claim could apply to a competitor without modification
- [ ] FAIL if adjectives appear without supporting evidence in the same sentence
- [ ] FAIL if the "reason why" is delayed past the second paragraph
- [ ] FAIL if the copy sounds clever — Hopkins regarded cleverness as a distraction
- [ ] FAIL if the offer appears without a risk-reduction element

---

## Style 5: John Caples

### Structural Requirements
- [ ] Headline reads like the opening of a story — creates curiosity about what happened
- [ ] The opening establishes a protagonist in a relatable, vulnerable situation
- [ ] A moment of social doubt or skepticism from others appears (someone doubted, laughed,
  or questioned)
- [ ] The transformation is shown, not just claimed — specific before/after details
- [ ] Future-pacing appears: the reader's life is described after using the product
- [ ] The reader is directly invited into the same story near the close

### Voice Characteristics
- [ ] Warm and hopeful — reads like a story told by a friend
- [ ] Present tense used during story sections to create immediacy
- [ ] Benefits are painted as images ("you'll sit down at any piano and hold the room
  spellbound") not abstractions ("you'll be a better musician")
- [ ] Sentence length varies between story sections (flowing) and benefit lists (punchy)
- [ ] Short questions used to re-engage at the start of new sections

### Failure Modes to Check
- [ ] FAIL if the headline is a benefit statement rather than a story hook
- [ ] FAIL if no social proof moment (doubt, skepticism, or recognition from others) appears
- [ ] FAIL if the transformation is abstract ("you'll feel more confident") rather than
  specific and visual
- [ ] FAIL if the reader is not directly invited into the story before the close
- [ ] FAIL if the opening establishes the product before establishing the protagonist

## Style 6: Joe Sugarman

### Structural Requirements
- [ ] First sentence is very short — ideally one to five words
- [ ] Headline creates a curiosity gap — does NOT resolve what it promises
- [ ] A story environment is established before the product is introduced
- [ ] Objections are addressed mid-copy, not saved for the end
- [ ] A trial close appears 60–70% through the copy, assuming the reader is already sold
- [ ] Technical specifications appear and are used as trust signals, not features

### Voice Characteristics
- [ ] Fast-paced — every sentence pulls to the next
- [ ] Warm and personally excitable — the writer cannot wait to tell you this
- [ ] Short paragraphs throughout — no paragraph exceeds 4 lines
- [ ] First-person, but the "I" always connects back to the reader's experience
- [ ] Humor appears naturally, not as a tactic

### Failure Modes to Check
- [ ] FAIL if the first paragraph is longer than three lines
- [ ] FAIL if the headline resolves its own curiosity gap
- [ ] FAIL if the product is introduced before the environment is established
- [ ] FAIL if no trial close appears before the final offer
- [ ] FAIL if technical specifications are absent — specs are a Sugarman trust signal,
  not a feature dump to be avoided

---

## Style 7: Gary Bencivenga

### Structural Requirements
- [ ] Headline contains or implies proof — a fact, result, number, or credible source
- [ ] Opening demonstrates precise empathy with the reader's situation
- [ ] Credibility of the source is established before any selling begins
- [ ] At least three distinct types of proof are stacked (e.g., study + testimonial + logical
  argument + demonstration + expert quote)
- [ ] The price is framed against the cost of inaction, not presented in isolation
- [ ] The guarantee is specific and used as a proof element, not just reassurance
- [ ] The close is calm — no pressure tactics; the proof does the persuasion

### Voice Characteristics
- [ ] Measured and authoritative — never hyped
- [ ] Longer average sentence length than other styles, but broken by short punchy statements
  at emotional peaks
- [ ] Parallel structures in proof sections build rhythm and accumulated weight
- [ ] Transitions are deliberate: "Which is exactly why..." / "And here's the proof"
- [ ] Warm without being casual — serious without being cold

### Failure Modes to Check
- [ ] FAIL if any superlative appears without evidence in the same sentence
- [ ] FAIL if fewer than three distinct proof types are present
- [ ] FAIL if the price appears without a cost-of-inaction frame nearby
- [ ] FAIL if any pressure tactic appears (countdown, "act now," scarcity without evidence)
- [ ] FAIL if the opening claims rather than empathizes — Bencivenga always makes the reader
  feel seen before he tries to sell them anything

---

## Scoring

**Pass:** All universal checks clear + all structural requirements met + 4/5 voice
characteristics present + 0–1 failure modes triggered.

**Revise:** Any universal check fails, OR 2+ failure modes triggered, OR fewer than 3
structural requirements met.

**Rewrite:** 3+ failure modes triggered, OR the copy reads as generic AI with a stylistic
label applied after the fact.

When in doubt: read the copy aloud. If it sounds like an AI trying to sound like a person,
it needs revision. If it sounds like a specific person writing to a specific reader, it's close.
